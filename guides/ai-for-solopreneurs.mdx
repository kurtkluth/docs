As a solopreneur, your most valuable resource is time. AI and automation let you multiply your output without hiring a team — handling content creation, client communication, lead nurturing, and reporting while you focus on high-value work. This guide covers the highest-ROI automation plays for one-person businesses, and shows you exactly how to get started building them today.

## The Top 5 Automations for Solopreneurs

These are the five automations that consistently deliver the biggest time savings for one-person businesses. Start with the one that maps closest to your biggest bottleneck.

1. **Content repurposing** — Recording a YouTube video or writing a blog post takes real effort. Once you have that core asset, an automation can extract the transcript, pass it through Claude or ChatGPT, and generate a Twitter thread, LinkedIn post, email newsletter snippet, and short-form video script — all without you writing a single word. One piece of content becomes five.

2. **Lead follow-up** — Speed is everything when a new lead comes in. An automation that fires within minutes of a form submission — pulling the lead's name, context, and intent from the form data and generating a personalized first-touch email via AI — consistently outperforms any manual follow-up you'll do three hours later. Connect it to your CRM and every new lead gets a warm response before you even know they exist.

3. **Client reporting** — Clients want to feel informed; you don't want to spend Friday afternoons writing status updates. Build a workflow that pulls data from your project management tool, ad platform, or analytics dashboard, feeds it to an AI model, and sends a formatted report on a schedule. Your clients get professional updates every week; you spend zero time producing them.

4. **Inbox management** — An unmanaged inbox is a tax on your attention. Use AI and automation to auto-label incoming emails by category (leads, clients, invoices, newsletters), generate one-line summaries for important threads, and route action items to a task manager. You check your inbox once a day and everything that matters is already sorted.

5. **Proposal generation** — When a discovery call ends and a prospect wants a proposal, the clock is ticking. Build a workflow triggered by your client intake form: it passes the form data to Claude with a proposal template prompt, generates a draft proposal, and drops it into a Google Doc or Notion page ready for your review. What used to take two hours takes ten minutes.

## Where to Start

<Steps>
  <Step title="Identify your most repetitive daily task">
    Spend five minutes writing down everything you do manually that happens the same way every time. Look for tasks you've done more than ten times this month. That repetition is your automation target — the higher the frequency, the bigger the return.
  </Step>

  <Step title="Map the steps">
    Before touching any tool, write out exactly what you do manually: what triggers the task, what data you need, what you do with that data, and where the output goes. This becomes your workflow blueprint. If you can describe it to a person, you can build it in n8n.
  </Step>

  <Step title="Build the workflow in n8n">
    Start simple. One trigger, one action. Don't try to build the perfect version on day one — build the version that works. A Gmail trigger that dumps emails into a Google Sheet is a real automation, even if it's not sophisticated. Ship it, use it, and improve from there.
  </Step>

  <Step title="Add AI">
    Once the basic workflow runs reliably, add a Claude or ChatGPT node to make it smarter. This is where the magic happens: the same data that was flowing through your workflow now gets summarized, rewritten, categorized, or responded to by an AI before the output is delivered.
  </Step>

  <Step title="Iterate">
    Activate the workflow and let it run for a week before you touch it again. Real usage reveals edge cases that testing never catches — missing fields, unexpected formats, scenarios you didn't anticipate. Each edge case you fix makes the workflow more robust. After a few iterations, most workflows run hands-off indefinitely.
  </Step>
</Steps>

<Tip>
  The best automation is the one you actually finish and run. Start with the task that annoys you most — the one you dread doing every week. You'll be far more motivated to push through the setup, and the relief when it runs itself will fuel every automation after it.
</Tip>
